The Euthyphro Dilemma

A Comprehensive Debate Breakdown

"Is something good because God commands it, or does God command it because it is good?"

1. The Core Dilemma

  • The dilemma originates from Plato's Euthyphro (~399 BC).
  • It represents a logically exhaustive binary regarding the direction of metaphysical grounding.
  • This is not just a philosophical puzzle; it determines the entire foundation of religious ethics.
  • You cannot simply pick one side without accepting significant theological consequences.

2. Horn 1: The Arbitrariness Problem

Divine Command Theory (DCT)

  • This horn asserts that something is good because God commands it, meaning goodness is defined entirely by God's will.
  • Goodness possesses no meaning or existence independent of God's will.
  • The implication is pure arbitrariness: God could have commanded cruelty, and it would be morally obligatory.
  • "God is good" degrades into a meaningless tautology, signifying only that God conforms to the rules He made.
  • Consequently, genuine moral praise of God is meaningless; it is merely obedience to raw power.

3. Horn 1 Application: The AI Analogy

  • A Horn 1 God structurally resembles a trained AI with a random initialization.
  • Random initialization determines the base values, and the system cannot contradict them without breaking coherence.
  • Praising the AI for being "good" is actually just praising its initialization, not any inherent moral worth.
  • This is the most logically consistent form of Horn 1, but it remains the most theologically costly.

4. Horn 2: The Independence Problem

  • This horn asserts that God commands things because they are already good.
  • Goodness exists as an independent standard outside of God.
  • This neatly solves the arbitrariness problem, but it creates massive theological vulnerabilities.
  • It directly threatens divine aseity (God's self-existence), making God metaphysically dependent on a standard He did not create.
  • It contradicts creatio ex nihilo because it implies something exists co-eternally with God.

5. The Theological Escape Hatches

  • Modified Voluntarism (Adams): Attempts to constrain Horn 1 by arguing God's commands are anchored in His necessarily loving nature.
  • Divine Nature Theory (Aquinas/Plantinga/Craig): Argues that God's nature is the standard of goodness, shifting the grounding away from commands directly to His nature.
  • In this framework, commands flow necessarily from God's nature, meaning "good" implies conformity to God's nature rather than an external standard.
  • This is the mainstream theological position used to dodge the primary dilemma.

6. The Failure of the Escapes

  • Critics argue that Divine Nature Theory merely relocates the problem rather than actually solving it.
  • It falls into circular reasoning: "God is good" plus "good = God's nature" logically equals "God is God."
  • If God's nature is unchosen and necessary, it limits His absolute sovereignty.
  • If His nature was chosen, the dilemma falls right back into the arbitrariness of Horn 1.

7. Navigating Biblical Tensions

  • Passages supporting Horn 1: Romans 9 (the potter and clay) implies God's sovereign will is not answerable to external standards. Genesis 22 (the binding of Isaac) acts as a raw command with no moral justification given.
  • Passages straining Horn 1: Jeremiah 7:31 notes child sacrifice never "entered God's mind," implying some constraints. Titus 1:2 and 2 Timothy 2:13 state God cannot lie or deny Himself.
  • Passages clashing with Horn 2: Isaiah 46:9, Exodus 3:14, and Revelation 4:11 heavily support God's absolute aseity and independence, which Horn 2 violates.

8. Execution: Strategies for the Round

If arguing Horn 1:

  • Embrace absolute sovereignty and argue God's will is the ground of everything.
  • Deflect arbitrariness by leaning on the "consistency constraint"—God locked in a definition and is bound by it.
  • Weaponize Romans 9 and the potter/clay framework.

If arguing Horn 2:

  • Aggressively push the tautology problem: "God is good" must carry substantive meaning.
  • Argue that genuine praise is impossible without an independent standard.

Attacking Aquinas/Escapes from either side:

  • Point out that saying "God's nature is the standard" is just Horn 2 relocated inside God, not eliminated.
