The Euthyphro Dilemma

Is something good because God commands it, or does God command it because it is good?

Plato · Aquinas · Divine Command Theory · Divine Nature Theory

01 / 13
Origins & Context

The Dialogue of Euthyphro

"Is the pious loved by the gods because it is pious, or is it pious because it is loved by the gods?" — Socrates, in Plato's Euthyphro (~399 BC)
  • Setting: Socrates encounters Euthyphro outside an Athenian court. Euthyphro is prosecuting his own father for impiety. Socrates asks him to define "piety."
  • The Trap: Euthyphro claims piety is what the gods love. Socrates exposes a fatal ambiguity — a dilemma with two unacceptable horns.
  • Why it matters: The dilemma strikes at the foundation of any morality rooted in divine authority, and still drives contemporary debates in philosophy of religion.

The Question

The Structure of the Dilemma

"Does God command X because X is morally good, or is X morally good because God commands it?"
Horn 1
X is good because God commands it
→ Morality is arbitrary — God could command cruelty and it would be "good."
Horn 2
God commands X because it is good
→ Morality is independent of God. God becomes redundant to ethics.
The Core Problem: Either God's commands are constrained by an external moral standard (limiting divine omnipotence), or morality is whatever God wills (making ethics arbitrary). Both seem theologically unacceptable.
03 / 13
Horn 1 — The Arbitrariness Horn

"It is good because God commands it"

If God's commands create moral facts from scratch, then there was no moral reason for God to issue those commands rather than their opposites.

  • The Arbitrariness Objection: If God commanded torture of innocents, would it be obligatory? Horn 1 says yes — and that's monstrous.
  • The Changeable Morality Problem: If God changed His commands, morality would flip overnight. "Murder is wrong" becomes contingent, not necessary.
  • The Empty Praise Problem: "God is good" collapses into "God obeys His own rules" — a tautology with zero moral content. You cannot praise a dictator for conforming to laws he invented and could revoke. Worship becomes fear of power, not genuine admiration of goodness.
  • The Regress: If obedience to God is good, what makes obedience itself good? Either something prior to God's commands does — defeating Horn 1 — or "obedience is good because God commands obedience," which is circular.
Strengths of This Horn
Preserves divine sovereignty fully. God is the uncaused source of all being and value. Upholds the biblical picture of God as the supreme authority.
The Deepest Problem
William of Ockham accepted this horn fully. Most theists find it intolerable — not just because genocide could be holy on God's whim, but because it makes moral praise of God literally impossible. "God is good" would mean nothing more than "God is God."
04 / 13
Horn 2 — The Independence Horn

"God commands it because it is good"

If God recognizes and commands what is already good, then moral goodness is a standard prior to and independent of God's will.

  • Implication: God functions as a moral reporter, not a moral legislator.
  • Implication: In principle, humans could access moral truths without reference to God at all.
  • Implication: God is "subject" to morality, not its source — arguably undermining divine sovereignty.
Strengths of This Horn
Preserves moral realism. Explains why gratuitous cruelty is wrong even if God commanded it. Aligns with moral intuitions — allows secular ethics to be valid.
Weaknesses
Threatens divine aseity (God's absolute independence). If morality exists apart from God, why worship God for it? God becomes irrelevant to ethics.

Theological Ethics

Divine Command Theory ( DCT )

Pure DCT (Horn 1 Straight)
  • Core Claim: An act is morally obligatory if and only if God commands it. Morality is constituted entirely by divine will — no prior standard exists.
  • Proponents: William of Ockham, John Duns Scotus. Biblical basis: Genesis 22 (Abraham/Isaac) — God commanding what seems horrific suggests morality follows command, not prior reason.
  • Fully accepts the arbitrariness horn. God could have commanded cruelty. The position is internally consistent — and theologically catastrophic.
Modified Voluntarism (Robert Adams)
  • The Patch: God commands only what is consistent with His essentially loving nature. Arbitrariness is constrained — cruelty is off the table because love is essential to what God is.
  • Still DCT? Barely. Commands still do the grounding work, but nature constrains them. Critics say this quietly abandons voluntarism — if nature constrains commands, nature is doing the real work, not the commands.
  • The Regress: Why is a loving nature the moral standard rather than some other nature? Either love is independently good (Horn 2 smuggled back in) or it's arbitrary — arbitrariness relocated, not eliminated.
Key Distinction: Pure DCT grounds morality in God's commands. Modified Voluntarism tries to constrain commands via nature. Divine Nature Theory (DNT) drops commands entirely and grounds morality in God's nature alone. Each is a distinct position — they are often conflated, but the difference matters in rounds.

The Third Option

The Aquinas Escape

"Goodness does not depend on God's will but on His nature. God commands what is good because it is an expression of who He is — not because He recognizes an external standard." — Paraphrase of Thomas Aquinas, Summa Theologica (~1265)
  • The Move: Reject the assumption that the only options are (A) external moral standard or (B) arbitrary will. Locate morality in God's nature itself.
  • Key Distinction: God does not consult an external standard, nor does He arbitrarily decree. He is the standard. His commands flow necessarily from His essential character.
  • How it escapes Horn 1: Cruelty could never be commanded because God is essentially good — it is metaphysically impossible, not merely unlikely.
Critical question for debate: Does this actually escape the dilemma, or does it just relocate it? Critics argue the dilemma re-emerges: is God's nature good because of some further standard, or is "goodness" just defined as whatever God's nature is?

The Third Option — Scrutiny

Evaluating the Aquinas Escape

In Its Favor
  • ✦ Avoids both horns simultaneously
  • ✦ Preserves divine sovereignty and moral necessity
  • ✦ "God is good" becomes substantive, not tautological
  • ✦ Grounds the necessary truths of ethics in a necessary Being
Against It
  • ✦ Dilemma re-emerges at the level of God's nature
  • ✦ "Good = God's nature" risks circularity or redefinition
  • ✦ Cannot explain why God's nature is the moral terminus, not just that it is
  • ✦ Demands a full metaphysical account of how divine nature grounds moral necessity
Debate Note: The Aquinas escape is most powerful when paired with Divine Nature Theory. By itself, it needs a metaphysical framework to avoid circularity. The question becomes: what grounds the nature itself?

Alternative Framework

Divine Nature Theory ( DNT )

  • Core Claim: Moral properties are grounded in God's nature, not His commands. God is the exemplar of all moral goodness — goodness just is conformity to the divine nature.
  • Key Proponents: Thomas Aquinas, C.S. Lewis, William Lane Craig (contemporary), Brian Leftow.
  • Moral Ontology: Moral facts are necessary truths grounded in the metaphysically necessary being of God. "Torturing innocents is wrong" is as necessary as "2+2=4."
  • God's Commands: Commands are expressions of and consistent with God's nature, not arbitrary decrees. This fully detaches DNT from the arbitrariness horn.
  • Craig's Formulation: Without God as the foundation, there is no adequate grounding for objective moral facts. DNT provides the ontological anchor that naturalistic ethics cannot.

Divine Nature Theory — Scrutiny

Objections to DNT

  • The Re-Emergence Objection: Does the dilemma now apply to God's nature? Is God's nature good by some further standard, or is "good" just defined as "like God"? Either way we seem stuck.
  • The Conceptual Analysis Problem: How do we access or verify the content of God's nature? DNT may require a complete theology before it can deliver ethics — a substantial starting burden in debate.
  • The Euthyphro Regress: Whatever grounds God's nature faces the same dilemma again. One can always ask: is God's nature necessarily good by some standard, or is "good" simply defined by it?
  • The Grounding Challenge: Secular moral realism argues that moral facts could in principle be necessary without a personal ground — putting the burden on DNT to explain why personhood is essential to moral ontology.
Best Theist Reply: God's nature is the stopping point for grounding — just as the laws of logic are not grounded in something further. All grounding chains terminate somewhere; theists argue God's necessary nature is the most coherent terminus.

Head to Head

DCT vs. DNT

Divine Command Theory
  • ✦ Morality = what God commands
  • ✦ Grounds morality in God's will
  • ✦ Strong: divine sovereignty preserved
  • ✗ Weak: open to arbitrariness objection
  • ✗ Makes "God is good" a tautology
  • ✗ Morality could change if God's will did
Divine Nature Theory
  • ✦ Morality = conformity to God's nature
  • ✦ Grounds morality in God's necessary being
  • ✦ Strong: escapes arbitrariness, preserves praise
  • ✦ Morality is as necessary as God's own existence
  • ✗ Dilemma re-emerges for God's nature
  • ✗ Requires a metaphysical account of how nature grounds necessity
Consensus among contemporary theist philosophers: DNT (combined with Aquinas's third option) is the dominant response to the dilemma. DCT survives primarily in voluntarist traditions (Scotus, Ockham, some Reformed theology).

Preparation · Strategy

Key Arguments to Know

Against Divine Grounding
  • Press the arbitrariness horn: "Could God command genocide and make it obligatory?" Forces the theist to DCT or DNT.
  • Press the re-emergence: "Is God's nature good by some further standard?" Challenges DNT's claim to fully resolve the dilemma.
  • Deploy the grounding challenge: argue moral facts could be necessary without a personal ground, and demand DNT explain why personhood is required.
For Divine Grounding
  • Aquinas escape: reject the dichotomy. Morality is in God's nature, not His commands, not an external standard.
  • Necessary being objection: all grounding stops somewhere; God's nature is the most coherent terminus.
  • Turn the secular horn: without God, moral realism has its own grounding problem. Why think non-natural moral facts exist?

The Question Remains

Ante Iudicem Aeterna

"Before the eternal judge" — the Euthyphro Dilemma has persisted for 2,400 years because it strikes at something fundamental: the relationship between divinity and moral reality. Neither horn is comfortable. The debate is the point.
