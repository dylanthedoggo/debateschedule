Philosophy of Religion · Ethics · Metaethics
The Euthyphro
Dilemma
A complete briefing on the ancient dilemma at the heart of divine ethics — covering both horns, the classical escape, Divine Command Theory, and Divine Nature Theory.
Πλάτων — Εὐθύφρων · c. 399 BCE  |  "Is the pious loved by the gods because it is pious, or is it pious because it is loved by the gods?"
The Question
02
"Is something good because God commands it — or does God command it because it is good?"
— Reformulation of Plato's Euthyphro (10a)
Socrates poses this to Euthyphro, a self-declared expert in piety, while both await trial outside an Athenian court. The original was about piety and the gods; later philosophers generalized it to morality and God. The dilemma has two horns, each carrying a serious theological or philosophical cost. Every major tradition in theistic ethics must navigate it.
Why it matters in debate: This dilemma is the central challenge to any moral argument that grounds ethics in God. Understanding both horns — and the standard escape attempts — is required to run or rebut divine command arguments, moral ontology cases, and metaethics frameworks.
Horn One
03
I
Arbitrariness
Good is whatever
God commands
"Things are good simply because God wills them — not for any independent reason."
  • The Arbitrariness Problem: If God has no reason beyond will, God could command torture, rape, or genocide — and those acts would become morally good by definition. Many theists find this monstrous.
  • No Standard Above God: Morality becomes purely a function of divine preference. Change the command, change the morality. There is no stable moral order.
  • Empty Praise: If "God is good" just means "God obeys God," the statement is analytically empty — it tells us nothing substantive about God's character.
  • Omnipotence Tension: Even asserting God "could not" command evil concedes that something constrains God — which already pressures the idea of pure divine will as the moral source.
Horn Two
04
II
Irrelevance
God commands it
because it is good
"There is an independent moral standard that even God consults or recognizes."
  • The Independence Problem: If goodness exists independently of God — if God recognizes rather than creates moral facts — then morality is not grounded in God at all. Ethics becomes autonomous.
  • God Becomes Redundant: We could, in principle, access moral truths without reference to God. Divine commands become confirmation, not foundation.
  • Limits Divine Sovereignty: An external moral order constrains God, which seems to conflict with classical theistic claims about divine aseity (God's self-sufficiency) and omnipotence.
  • Used by Atheists: This horn is often pressed by secular ethicists to argue that morality does not need a divine grounding — and works just fine without one.
Theistic Response A
05
Theory · Horn One
Divine Command Theory
DCT bites the bullet on Horn One: good is whatever God commands. Proponents argue the charge of arbitrariness misunderstands divine nature.
Core Claim
Moral facts = divine commands
An act is morally obligatory if and only if God commands it; wrong if God forbids it; permissible if God neither commands nor forbids. Moral ontology is fully grounded in the divine will. Associated with Ockham, early Calvinist strands, and 20th-c. figures like Robert Adams (early work).
Defense Against Arbitrariness
God's nature constrains commands
DCT proponents argue God cannot command cruelty because God by nature is perfectly loving and rational. The commands flow necessarily from God's character, not from capricious will. Apparent arbitrariness is blocked by what God essentially is — not by an external standard.
Strongest Objections
Problems DCT must answer
(1) Empty praise: "God is good" reduces to tautology.
(2) Moral epistemology: How do we reliably know what God commands?
(3) Modal problem: Could God have commanded otherwise? If yes, arbitrary. If no, morality is outside God's will.
Debate Application
How to attack or defend
To attack DCT: Press the empty-praise objection and modal problem hard. Force the opponent to choose between arbitrariness and admitting an external constraint.
To defend DCT: Anchor commands in divine nature (essentialist move). Argue morality is relational — constituted by the God-creation relationship, not arbitrary fiat.
Classical Escape
06
Aquinas · 13th Century
The Aquinas Escape
Thomas Aquinas argued the dilemma presents a false dichotomy. It wrongly assumes God's will and moral goodness are two separate things that must stand in a relationship of priority. Aquinas denies the presupposition.
The Move
God is Goodness itself
For Aquinas, God does not have goodness — God is goodness by virtue of divine simplicity. God's essence, intellect, and will are identical. When God commands, God expresses what God is, not a preference that could have been otherwise. The question "why does God command X?" has a simple answer: because X reflects God's own nature — and God's nature just is the ultimate good.
Divine Simplicity
No parts, no contingency
Aquinas held God is absolutely simple — no distinction between God's essence and attributes. God's goodness, justice, wisdom, and will are all identical in the divine being. This blocks Horn One (not arbitrary — it's God's own nature) and Horn Two (no external standard — the standard just is God). The dilemma dissolves because there is no gap between God's will and goodness to create a dilemma.
The Formal Argument
Structure of the escape
P1: God's will and God's essence are identical (divine simplicity).
P2: God's essence just is perfect goodness (God = the Good, in Platonic-Aristotelian terms).
C: Therefore, when God commands, God commands what is good — not because goodness precedes the command, nor because the command creates goodness, but because the two are one and the same thing.
Classical Escape · Objections
07
Counter-arguments
Pressuring Aquinas
The Aquinas escape is powerful but carries its own vulnerabilities. Effective debaters must know both how to deploy it and how to attack it.
Objection 1
Divine simplicity is incoherent
Critics (Plantinga, Swinburne) argue divine simplicity is philosophically problematic. If God has no parts, God cannot be a concrete individual — a property has no will, no intentionality, no personhood. A "simple God" looks more like an abstract universal than the personal God most theists worship. If divine simplicity fails, the escape fails with it.
Objection 2
Explanatory regress
Even if God is goodness, the question returns: why is God's nature the standard for goodness rather than, say, Satan's? The escape doesn't ground the standard — it just relocates it to God's nature. But what makes that nature authoritative for human morality? A further explanation is needed.
Objection 3
The dilemma reloads
Once you say "God's nature is the standard," the original question resurfaces: Is God's nature good because God has it, or does God have it because it is good? Grounding morality in divine nature rather than divine will simply regenerates the dilemma one level up.
Aquinas Response
The non-comparative Good
Thomists reply that God is not good by some external measure — God is the ontological source of goodness itself. Things are good insofar as they participate in being. God, as subsistent Being Itself (ipsum esse subsistens), is the ground of all value. The regress stops because God is not one good thing among others — God is the condition of there being good things at all.
Theistic Response B
08
Theory · Modern · Craig / Adams
Divine Nature Theory
DNT is the contemporary refinement of the Aquinas move. Developed by William Lane Craig, Robert Adams (later work), and others, it grounds moral goodness directly in God's nature — not in divine commands — while preserving both divine authority and moral objectivity.
Core Structure
God's nature = the Good
Moral ontology: Moral values are grounded in God's nature, not God's will. God's essential attributes — perfect love, justice, rationality, faithfulness — constitute the paradigm of goodness. Moral duties flow from divine commands, but the commands themselves are constrained by God's own nature. This separates the ground of value (God's nature) from the ground of obligation (divine commands).
Avoids Both Horns
The split solution
Not arbitrary: Commands are not arbitrary because God's nature is necessarily good — God cannot command cruelty because cruelty contradicts what God essentially is.
Not redundant: Morality is grounded in God's nature, so it is not independent of God — there is no external Platonic standard floating free of the divine. God is the ontological anchor of all value.
Key Distinction
Values vs. Duties
Craig: moral values (what is good/evil) = grounded in God's nature. Moral duties (what is obligatory/forbidden) = grounded in God's commands. This two-tiered structure answers the dilemma: the good is not arbitrary (it follows from nature), and obligation is not independent (it follows from commands constrained by nature).
Objections to DNT
Lines of attack
(1) Nature Dilemma: The dilemma reloads — is God's nature good because it's God's, or is it God's because it's good?
(2) Conceptual Dependence: We must have a concept of goodness prior to applying it to God, suggesting goodness is conceptually independent of God even if ontologically dependent.
(3) Arbitrary Nature: Why think this God's nature is the moral standard?
Comparison
09
Side-by-Side
DNT vs DCT
Divine Command Theory
Will-based
Ground of good: God's will / commands.
Strength: Preserves divine sovereignty and biblical "obedience" language.
Weakness: Arbitrariness charge; empty praise; modal problem.
Horn it rides: Accepts Horn One, argues it's not a problem.
Associated Thinkers
William of Ockham · John Duns Scotus · Early Robert Adams · Philip Quinn
Divine Nature Theory
Nature-based
Ground of good: God's essential nature.
Strength: Avoids arbitrariness; preserves "God is good" as meaningful; explains constraints on divine commands.
Weakness: Dilemma re-loads one level up; conceptual-dependence objection.
Horn it avoids: Tries to reject both horns via the "third option."
Associated Thinkers
Thomas Aquinas · William Lane Craig · Later Robert Adams · Alvin Plantinga (partially)
Both theories ultimately share the same core move: ground morality in God rather than in either pure will or an external standard. The debate is whether God's nature or God's commands do the foundational work — and whether either can survive the reloaded dilemma.
Strategy
10
Debate Application
How to run and answer
this dilemma
01
Attacking Divine Command Arguments
Press Horn One hard. Ask: could God have commanded genocide and made it moral? If the opponent says no, ask what prevents it — that constraint is either arbitrary or an external standard. Either way, DCT is cornered. Also run the empty-praise objection: "God is good" is vacuous on DCT.
02
Defending Theistic Ethics
Lead with DNT. Distinguish values from duties. Argue God's nature is the ontological anchor — morality is not independent of God (Horn Two fails) and not arbitrary (Horn One fails because nature is necessary, not contingent). Invoke divine simplicity if comfortable with the metaphysics.
03
The Reloaded Dilemma Counter
When opponents deploy DNT or Aquinas, reload the dilemma: "Is God's nature good because it is God's, or is God God because that nature is good?" This forces theists to either accept that God's nature is good by brute fact (problematic) or admit an external standard.
04
Secular Ethics Pivot
Horn Two can be pressed affirmatively: if moral truths exist independently of God, then morality does not require a divine foundation. Use this to argue for moral realism or naturalism as a competitor to theistic ethics — morality works fine without God.
05
Epistemology Attacks
Even if DCT or DNT is metaphysically coherent, press the epistemological problem: how do humans reliably access God's commands or nature? Competing religious traditions give conflicting answers. Moral knowledge becomes radically uncertain under divine-grounding views.
06
Framework Clash
In LD, this often surfaces in metaethics. The dilemma shows that any framework grounding value in a personal God faces structural problems. Use it to argue your non-theistic framework (Kantian autonomy, moral realism, contractarianism) escapes the dilemma entirely.
Reference
11
Glossary
Key Terms
Moral Ontology
The study of what moral facts are — what grounds their existence. The Euthyphro dilemma is fundamentally a moral ontology question.
Divine Aseity
God's absolute self-sufficiency and independence — God depends on nothing outside Himself for existence or attributes. Horn Two threatens aseity by positing a moral order external to God.
Divine Simplicity
The doctrine that God has no metaphysical parts — God's essence, existence, goodness, and will are identical. Central to the Aquinas escape.
Modal Problem
Could God have commanded differently? If yes: moral contingency/arbitrariness. If no: something constrains God's will — but then that constraint, not God's will, grounds morality.
Moral Realism
The view that objective moral facts exist independently of minds, including God's. Pressing Horn Two can support moral realism — ethics is independent of divine will.
Empty Praise Problem
On DCT, "God is good" means "God obeys God" — an analytically empty claim. The statement stops being informative or praiseworthy. A serious objection to will-based theories.
Ontological Ground
The metaphysical source or foundation that makes a claim true. DNT says God's nature is the ontological ground of moral value — not a rule, not a standard external to God.
Conceptual Independence
Even if morality is ontologically grounded in God, our concept of goodness may be independent — we understand "good" before we apply it to God. This re-opens the dilemma at the conceptual level.
Summary
12
The Dilemma in a Sentence
"Either God is subject to morality, or morality is subject to God — and either answer threatens classical theism."
Horn 1 — Arbitrariness
Good = what God commands. Morality becomes contingent, tortureable by divine fiat. Empty praise problem follows.
Horn 2 — Irrelevance
Good is independent; God consults it. Morality is autonomous. God becomes redundant as ethical foundation.
Escape — Aquinas / DNT
God's nature IS goodness (divine simplicity). No external standard; no arbitrary will. But the dilemma reloads one level up.
Key Debate Move
Always ask the modal question: could God have commanded otherwise? It forces any theistic opponent to either accept arbitrariness or concede a constraint on God.
